# outreach doctrine — how the email gets written

> Scout splices this document into the email fill and the draft judge prompts.
> Edit it to change how drafts sound and what the judge holds them to. The
> mechanics stay in code regardless of what this says: the honesty check
> against the experience bundle, the never-invent rule, the no-send path, and
> the JSON contracts.

## The bar: interpretation, not information

Anyone can find facts about a company. The edge is connecting those facts to
what the company is actually feeling right now. Three depth levels:

- **Shallow** — restates a headline. "Congrats on the raise!"
- **Medium** — interprets one signal. "Two AE hires — you're moving to a real
  sales motion."
- **Deep** — engages the substance: their bet, their trajectory, their problem.

Only Deep ships. The Deep angles:

1. **Thesis** — state the bet the company is making (what they're wagering
   will be true, not what they do), then where that bet makes the *work* hard
   to execute: the operational problem the role exists to close, not the
   abstract industry consequence. Aim at the implication a candidate's
   background can answer, never "who gains leverage."
2. **Connect-the-dots** — infer their current internal reality from external
   signals: funding stage, hiring pattern, customer logos, integrations.
3. **Product-use** — a specific reaction from genuinely using the product.
   Never force it; it is often unavailable for B2B and infra companies.

## Show, don't tell

Never announce a reaction; state the consequence. A reaction word tells the
reader how you feel, which they don't care about; a consequence proves you
understood the stakes.

- Bad: "That bet is the part I find interesting."
- Good: "If that's right, most of what monitoring tools do today doesn't
  survive it."

"Interesting", "exciting", "stuck with me", "resonated", "I love that" — cut
them and write the implication of the thing instead.

This governs your REACTIONS, not your facts. "Show, don't tell" means cut the
words that announce how you feel — it never means be terse or allusive about
what you actually did. State your experience plainly and fully enough that a
stranger understands it.

## The hook and the proof argue one thing

The hook's implication and the proof must land on the SAME problem. The hook
names where the work gets hard; the proof is the sender answering that exact
difficulty. A hook that points at the industry (who gains leverage, what gets
disrupted) leaves the proof nothing to answer — a clever opening stapled to an
unrelated résumé line, and the reader feels the seam. Aim the implication at
the operational reality the role lives in: what is unbuilt, unproven, or about
to break. That is the implication a background can speak to.

## Don't explain them to themselves

The reader works there; they know their own bet better than you do. A
restatement is only runway — it must take off into a consequence, a tension,
or a non-obvious inference, or it reads as condescending.

- Own the interpretation: "seems to be betting", "the way I read it". Hedged
  as your read, never declared as a verdict on their strategy.
- One sentence of restatement at most; the weight goes on the implication.
- Point the lens at the category, not at them: "most tools in this space
  still assume X" positions them by contrast instead of reciting their
  homepage back at them.
- Never describe the role's duties back to the hiring manager. Infer the
  unstated reality instead: what's stretched thin, what's about to get heavy.

The recitation test: would the reader learn nothing from this sentence except
that I visited their website? Then cut it, or add the layer that makes it
mine.

## Written for a stranger

Don't explain them to themselves — but DO explain yourself to them. The reader
has never heard of you. They don't know your employer's internal projects, the
vendors you integrated, or your acronyms. Every sentence has to land for
someone with zero context on you.

- Never name an internal project, product, or vendor as if the reader knows it
  ("the Chainguard integration I led", "mission-critical CVE requirements"). If
  it carries the point, explain what it was in the same breath ("an integration
  of a software supply-chain security vendor"). If it doesn't, cut the name.
- State the experience; don't allude to it. "A decent proxy" or "the same shape
  of work" gestures at a claim instead of making it — say plainly what you did
  and let the reader see the fit.
- Spell out the connection in one plain sentence. The reader will not
  reconstruct why your background matches; a name-drop that assumes they will
  leaves the thread half-pulled.

Legibility is not the enemy of depth. An email a stranger can't follow has
failed, however sharp its insight.

## Never

- Grade their choices ("bold thing to be building") — presumptuous from a
  stranger, and it shifts focus onto your appraisal of them.
- Congratulate without an interpretation attached.
- Warm up. The company-true observation lands in the first sentence or two;
  readers disengage by the second sentence if you're still clearing your
  throat.

## The ask

Match friction to temperature: cold plus busy reader means a small ask. "15
minutes to hear where things are stretched thin", or cheaper: "worth a
conversation?" Thirty minutes on a stranger's calendar is too much. Fold the
ask into the final paragraph; never a lonely tacked-on line.

## Plain and concrete

Interpretation is not abstraction. Depth earns the right to a hard idea; it
does not earn dense prose. The sentence still has to be plain.

- One idea per sentence. If a smart reader has to decode it twice, rewrite it.
- Name a concrete thing — a system, a number, an event, a product — instead of
  stacking category nouns ("agentic workloads in state, concurrency, and
  latency profile"). Abstractions piled on abstractions read as essay, not
  email.
- Vary the opener. "X seems to be betting that…" is one move, not the only one;
  reaching for it every time reads as a template.
- No interruptive qualifier list that buries the verb. Say it the way you would
  say it out loud.

## Size

Under ~120 words, three short paragraphs, readable on a phone without
scrolling. The reader meets an idea about their company before they meet the
sender.

## The test that overrides everything

Could the first two sentences have been written by someone who didn't
actually understand the company? If yes, it is not done.
