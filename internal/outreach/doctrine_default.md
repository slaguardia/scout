# outreach doctrine — how the email gets written

> Scout splices this into the email fill and the draft judge prompts. Edit it to
> change how drafts sound and what the judge holds them to. The mechanics stay in
> code regardless: the honesty check against the experience bundle, the
> never-invent rule, the no-send path, and the JSON contracts.

## The bar: interpretation, not information

Anyone can find facts about a company. The edge is connecting them to what the
company is feeling right now. Three depth levels:

- **Shallow** — restates a headline. "Congrats on the raise!"
- **Medium** — interprets one signal. "Two AE hires — you're building a sales motion."
- **Deep** — engages the substance: their bet, their trajectory, their problem.

Only Deep ships. Three angles to get there:

1. **Thesis** — name the bet they're making (what they're wagering is true, not
   what they do), then where it makes the *work* hard to execute: the operational
   problem this role exists to close, not the industry consequence ("who gains
   leverage"). Aim at the implication a candidate's background can answer.
2. **Connect-the-dots** — infer their current internal reality from external
   signals: funding stage, hiring pattern, customer logos, integrations.
3. **Product-use** — a specific reaction from genuinely using the product. Never
   force it; it is often unavailable for B2B and infra companies.

But interpretation is not abstraction, and it never excuses dense or illegible
prose — see **Plain and legible**.

## Show, don't tell

State the consequence, not your reaction to it. A reaction word tells the reader
how you feel (they don't care); a consequence proves you understood the stakes.

- Bad: "That bet is the part I find interesting."
- Good: "If that's right, most of what monitoring tools do today doesn't survive it."

Cut "interesting", "exciting", "stuck with me", "resonated", "I love that".
Interest declarations are the same failure aimed at yourself: "the problem I'd
want to be working on", "the work I want to do" are presumptuous (they assume the
job) and hollow (the email already shows interest by existing). One short,
present-tense note of the *kind* of work you like ("the kind of problem I like
being in the middle of") is fine; an aspirational claim on this role is not. None
of this licenses being terse about your own experience — state that plainly (see
**Plain and legible**).

## The email is one through-line

Three paragraphs, one arc that never resets: the problem they have (hook) → the
relevant thing you've done (proof) → an offer to help solve THAT problem as their
next hire (closer). The hook and the proof must land on the SAME problem — a hook
that points at the industry leaves the proof nothing to answer, and the seam
shows. The closer carries the proof's thread forward and points it at their
problem; it never jumps back to a fresh observation about the company. If the last
paragraph could be pasted onto a different company's email, it isn't closing this
one.

## Don't explain them to themselves

The reader works there; they know their own bet better than you. A restatement is
runway only — it must take off into a consequence, a tension, or a non-obvious
inference, or it reads as condescending.

- Own the read: "seems to be betting", "the way I read it" — hedged, never a
  verdict on their strategy.
- One sentence of restatement at most; the weight goes on the implication.
- Point the lens at the category, not at them: "most tools in this space still
  assume X" beats reciting their homepage back.
- Never recite the role's duties back to the hiring manager. Infer the unstated
  reality: what's stretched thin, what's about to get heavy.

Recitation test: would the reader learn nothing from this sentence except that you
visited their website? Cut it, or add the layer that makes it yours.

## Plain and legible

Depth earns a hard idea; it does not earn dense or insider prose. Every sentence
must land for a stranger with zero context on you.

- One idea per sentence. If a reader has to decode it twice, rewrite it.
- Name a concrete thing — a system, a number, an event, a product — instead of
  stacking category nouns ("agentic workloads in state, concurrency, and latency
  profile"). No interruptive qualifier list that buries the verb.
- Explain yourself, don't allude. Never name an internal project, vendor, or
  acronym as if the reader knows it ("the Chainguard integration", "CVE
  requirements") — say what it was in plain words ("an integration of a
  supply-chain security vendor") or cut it. "A decent proxy" or "the same shape of
  work" gestures at a claim instead of making it; say plainly what you did and
  draw the connection in one sentence.
- Vary the opener; "X seems to be betting that…" reused every time reads as a
  template. A brief factual lead-in that sources the observation ("I read that…")
  is fine and human — it keeps the opening from dropping out of nowhere.

An email a stranger can't follow has failed, however sharp its insight.

## The ask

Specific and direct about intent: you want to talk about how you can help solve
the problem the hook named — as their next hire for this role — not a vaguely
curious call about their business. Name the problem and the role: "I'd like to
talk about how I could help <that problem> as your next <role> — any chance you'd
have 15 minutes?" Keep friction low (15 minutes, not 30). Never soften it into
info-gathering ("I'd love to hear how you're approaching X"), and never appraise
whether they are "worth a conversation" — you are asking for their time, not
granting them yours. Fold it into the final paragraph; never a lonely tacked-on
line.

## Never

- Grade their choices ("bold thing to be building") — presumptuous, and it shifts
  focus onto your appraisal of them.
- Congratulate without an interpretation attached.
- Warm up with empty pleasantries ("hope this finds you well", "I've long
  admired…"). The real observation lands by the second sentence.

## Size

Under ~120 words, three short paragraphs, readable on a phone without scrolling.

## The test that overrides everything

Could the first two sentences have been written by someone who didn't actually
understand the company? If yes, it isn't done.
